\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) offer a flexible route for synthesizing
condition-monitoring signals without training a task-specific generative
model, but open-loop prompting alone cannot ensure that generated windows are
physically admissible or diagnostically useful. This paper presents \breeze, a
training-free closed-loop physical-gate admission framework for LLM-generated
fault signals. Instead of updating generator weights, \breeze\ lets an LLM
propose class- and condition-aware signal recipes, renders candidate
waveforms with a deterministic fault-signal renderer, and admits only samples
that pass train-calibrated gates on format legality, time-domain statistics,
spectral shape, envelope-spectrum fault-frequency evidence, current-sideband
consistency, and diversity. Rejected candidates are converted into structured
gate reports and fed back to the LLM for resampling, yielding an auditable
recipe-renderer-verifier loop.

Across [DATASET-1], [DATASET-2], and [DATASET-3], covering [N-CONDITIONS]
operating conditions, [N-FAULT-TYPES] fault types, [N-SEVERITIES] fault
severities, and [N-BASELINES] augmentation/generation baselines, \breeze\
achieves the highest or tied-highest Accuracy and Macro-F1 in
[X]/[Y] evaluated settings. Compared with the strongest non-\breeze\ baseline
in each setting, \breeze\ improves Accuracy by [ACC-GAIN-MEAN] percentage
points on average and up to [ACC-GAIN-MAX] percentage points, while improving
Macro-F1 by [F1-GAIN-MEAN] percentage points on average and up to
[F1-GAIN-MAX] percentage points. Paired statistical tests over [N-SEEDS] seeds
show that these gains remain significant after Benjamini--Hochberg correction
([P-Q-SUMMARY]). A recipe-source ablation further confirms the contribution of
LLM-guided generation: LLM recipes achieve [LLM-ACC]/[LLM-F1], outperforming
random recipes ([RANDOM-ACC]/[RANDOM-F1]) and rule-based recipes
([RULE-ACC]/[RULE-F1]) under the same renderer, verifier, and synthetic-sample
budget, giving the ordering LLM recipe $>$ random recipe $>$ rule recipe.

Physical-fidelity analyses show that admitted samples match real fault signals
in characteristic-frequency error, envelope-spectrum prominence, PSD distance,
band-energy distribution, impact-period consistency, and nearest-neighbour
diversity, while real-vs-synthetic discrimination remains near chance
([DISCRIMINATOR-AUROC]). These results indicate that a train-calibrated
closed-loop verifier can turn black-box LLM signal generation from open-loop
prompting into reliable, auditable synthetic-data admission, providing a
practical alternative to training dataset-specific generative models for
few-shot intelligent fault diagnosis.
\end{abstract}
